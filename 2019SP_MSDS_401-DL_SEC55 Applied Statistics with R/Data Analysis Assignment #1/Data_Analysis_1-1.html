If you were presented with an overall histogram and summary statistics from a sample of some population or phenomenon and no other information, what questions might you ask before accepting them as representative of the sampled population or phenomenon?

Answer: Answer: I would ask the following questions:
Distribution of the data and sampling errors: What sampling method was used and how was it obtained? How accurately is this sample represent the total population? How well the data distributed? How big are a population and sample data? Are they any skewness of the data? What do outliers mean and how should we interpret them?
Time factor and incidents: How long ago was the study conducted and is it still relevant? Does anything change after the study? After the study conducted, were there any major incidents (oil spills, disease spread) or natural disasters which can change the studied population? After the study conducted, are there any ecosystem changes such as warming ocean temperature or migration of other sea animals that either compete area or harm the studied creature?
Data modification and non-sampling errors: Are there any missing data, recording error, measurement error, input processing error, analysis error, response error, or other factors? Were there any data cleaning, alterations, additions, classifications/groupings, or modifications happened?

Essay Question 3) (3 points) Do not refer to the abalone data or study. What do you see as difficulties analyzing data derived from observational studies? Can causality be determined? What might be learned from such studies?

Answer: In an observational study, we measure or survey members of a sample without trying to affect them. Like experiments, observational studies attempt to understand cause-and-effect relationships. We need to investigate the connection or correlation of two variables and find the true cause. Are both correlated variables related to a third, unknown factor? What is the third, unknown factor? Is the third factor hidden to sell the idea? We need to ask further follow-up questions: Who conducted or funded the study? Is the study biased by the researcher? What is the purpose of the study? Does the correlation indicate the reverse causation, indirect causation, coincidence or other types of causations? We should not jump to a conclusion when we find the connection or correlation between two variables.
